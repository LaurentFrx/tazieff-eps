## Why Safety First?

In strength training, incorrect technique under load can cause serious injuries (back, knees, shoulders). These 6 principles apply to **every exercise, every set, every rep** — regardless of the load.

> **Absolute rule:** if you can't maintain these principles, the load is too heavy. Reduce it.

---

## Principle 1 — Optimal Back Positioning

> **Keep a flat back or full contact with the support depending on the exercise.**

The back is the most injury-prone area in strength training. Two reference positions:

**Flat back (neutral spine)** — standing or free-weight exercises:
- Spine in neutral position (neither too arched nor too rounded)
- Active core bracing at all times
- Examples: squat, deadlift, dumbbell rows

**Back in contact with the support** — lying or seated exercises:
- Entire back pressed against the bench or seat
- No excessive lower back arch
- Examples: bench press, leg press, seated curl

❌ **Never do this:** round your lower back under load, release core bracing at the end of a fatigued set.

---

## Principle 2 — Mastering the Breathing Pattern

> **Exhale on effort, inhale on recovery.**

Breathing plays a key role in trunk stabilization and joint safety.

**General rule:**
- **Inhale** → eccentric phase (lowering, stretching)
- **Exhale** → concentric phase (push, pull, effort)

**Exception — intense core bracing and heavy loads:**
Take a deep breath before the rep (partial Valsalva maneuver) to create stabilizing intra-abdominal pressure, then exhale in a controlled manner during the effort.

❌ **Avoid:** completely holding your breath for multiple reps (risk of fainting) or breathing erratically.

---

## Principle 3 — Respecting Joint Angles

> **Limit joint angles to 90° to avoid excessive tendon stress.**

Each joint has a safe working range. Exceeding recommended angles under load greatly increases the risk of tendon and ligament injuries.

**Knee (squat, lunges, leg press):**
- Descend to 90° maximum (thighs parallel to the floor)
- Knees aligned with feet — don't let them cave inward

**Elbow (bench press, curl):**
- Don't hyperextend the elbow at the end of the movement
- Full but controlled range of motion

**Shoulder (press, raises):**
- Elbows below shoulders — not behind the line of your back
- Avoid painful closed angles

❌ **Never do this:** go lower than necessary to "impress", or let your knees cave in during squats.

---

## Principle 4 — Strategic Gaze Orientation

> **Look straight ahead to naturally maintain a straight back.**

Your gaze guides your spine. A well-placed gaze automatically promotes good back positioning.

**General rule:** look straight ahead, slightly above horizontal.

- **Squat, deadlift** → horizontal gaze, looking forward (neither too high, nor down)
- **Bench press** → look at the ceiling, eyes under the bar
- **Rows** → look at the floor, about 45° in front of you

❌ **Avoid:** looking at your feet (causes trunk flexion), forcefully looking up (compresses the cervical spine).

---

## Principle 5 — Using a Squat Wedge

> **Elevate the heels to protect the ankle joint and tendons.**

Ankle mobility often limits the depth and technical quality of the squat. Using a wedge (or elevated heels) allows you to:
- Keep a more upright torso
- Squat deeper without lifting your heels
- Reduce stress on the knees and lower back

**When to use a wedge:**
- If your heels lift before reaching 90° of knee flexion
- If your torso leans excessively forward
- During the learning phase to consolidate technique

❌ **It's not a weakness**: the wedge is an educational and preventive tool, not a crutch.

---

## Principle 6 — The Spotter's Role

> **Observe, count, advise, protect.**

A spotter is essential for heavy free-weight exercises. Their role evolves with experience level:

**Year 10 — Responsible practitioner:**
- Handle weights and equipment with care
- Put stations back in order after use
- Stay attentive to your partner

**Year 11 — Observer partner:**
- Count reps
- Observe and advise on technique
- Help optimize the load

**Year 12 — Expert partner:**
- Count reps and optimize load in real time
- Continuously identify shortcomings in range of motion and safe postures
- Provide relevant and targeted advice

**For high-risk exercises (heavy bench press, heavy squat):**
The spotter positions themselves ready to assist, hands under the bar, prepared to intervene without being asked if muscle failure occurs.
