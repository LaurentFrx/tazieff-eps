## Why Learn About Your Muscles?

Training without knowing your muscles is like driving without a map. Knowing **which muscle you're working**, **where it's located**, and **what its opposite is** allows you to build balanced sessions, avoid injuries, and progress faster.

---

## Main Muscles

### Anterior (Front of the Body)

| Muscle | Area | Main Role |
|--------|------|-----------|
| **Pectorals** | Chest | Horizontal push (bench press) |
| **Biceps brachii** | Front of arm | Elbow flexion (curl) |
| **Brachioradialis** | Forearm | Elbow flexion, supination |
| **Serratus anterior** | Side of ribcage | Shoulder stabilization |
| **Rectus abdominis** | Abdomen | Trunk flexion, core bracing |
| **Obliques** | Sides | Rotation and lateral flexion |
| **Quadriceps** | Front of thigh | Knee extension (squat, leg press) |
| **Adductors** | Inner thigh | Bringing legs together |
| **Tibialis anterior** | Shin | Dorsiflexion of the foot |

### Posterior (Back of the Body)

| Muscle | Area | Main Role |
|--------|------|-----------|
| **Trapezius** | Neck/upper back | Shoulder elevation and retraction |
| **Deltoids** | Shoulders | Arm elevation (shoulder press) |
| **Infraspinatus, Teres minor & major, Rhomboids** | Upper back | External rotation, scapular retraction |
| **Latissimus dorsi** | Back | Vertical and horizontal pulling |
| **Triceps** | Back of arm | Elbow extension |
| **Quadratus lumborum / Erector spinae** | Lower back | Spinal stabilization |
| **Gluteus maximus** | Glutes | Hip extension (hip thrust, squat) |
| **Gluteus medius** | Side of glutes | Hip abduction, stabilization |
| **Hamstrings** | Back of thigh | Knee flexion, hip extension |
| **Triceps surae** (Gastrocnemius + Soleus) | Calves | Plantar flexion (calf raise) |

> **Year 10 Goal**: know and locate at least **10 muscles** from this list.

## Agonist and Antagonist

Every movement involves two types of muscles working in opposition:

**The agonist muscle** produces the main movement — it contracts.
**The antagonist muscle** opposes and slows the movement — it relaxes.

| Movement | Agonist | Antagonist |
|----------|---------|------------|
| Elbow flexion (curl) | Biceps | Triceps |
| Elbow extension | Triceps | Biceps |
| Knee extension (squat) | Quadriceps | Hamstrings |
| Knee flexion | Hamstrings | Quadriceps |
| Horizontal push | Pectorals | Latissimus dorsi |
| Horizontal row | Latissimus dorsi | Pectorals |

> **Golden rule**: always work both sides of a joint. Training biceps without training triceps creates a muscular imbalance that promotes injuries.

## Isolation vs Compound Exercises

### Isolation Exercise
Targets **a single muscle** using **a single joint**.
- Examples: biceps curl, leg extension, leg curl, dumbbell fly
- Best for: targeting a specific muscle, finishing a session, correcting an imbalance

### Compound Exercise
Engages **multiple muscles** through **multiple joints simultaneously**.
- Examples: squat, bench press, pull-ups, rows, deadlift
- Best for: maximum energy expenditure, overall strength, session foundation

> **Strategy**: start your session with compound exercises (when you're fresh), finish with isolation exercises.

---

## Muscular Balance — A Fundamental Principle

To progress without getting injured, maintain 3 balances:

**1. Agonists / Antagonists**
Always work both sides of a joint within the week.
Examples: Biceps ↔ Triceps · Quadriceps ↔ Hamstrings · Pectorals ↔ Latissimus dorsi

**2. Core Stabilizers**
Work both the anterior (abdominals) AND posterior (quadratus lumborum, erector spinae) of the trunk to protect the spine.

**3. Upper Body / Lower Body**
Don't neglect the lower body in favor of the upper body — the legs represent the largest muscle mass in the body.

---

## Key Takeaways for Your Training Log

Before choosing your exercises, ask yourself these 3 questions:
1. Which muscle do I want to target?
2. Have I worked its antagonist this week?
3. Have I planned exercises for core stabilizers?
