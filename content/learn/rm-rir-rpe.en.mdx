## Why Measure Intensity?

In strength training, working "hard enough" isn't enough. To progress, you need to know and control your **intensity level** for every set. Three complementary tools exist for this: RM, RIR, and RPE.

---

## RM — Maximum Repetition

> **The maximum load you can lift for a specific number of repetitions.**

### Definition
RM (Maximum Repetition) is an **objective measure** of your maximum strength on a given exercise.

- **1RM** = the maximum load you can lift **once** with correct technique
- **5RM** = the maximum load you can lift **5 times**
- **10RM** = the maximum load you can lift **10 times**

### What Is It For?
RM is used to **calculate your working loads** as a percentage of your maximum strength.

| Goal | Intensity | Approx. Reps |
|------|-----------|---------------|
| Strength endurance | 50–70% of 1RM | 12–20 reps |
| Muscle growth | 65–80% of 1RM | 8–12 reps |
| Power gain | 80–95% of 1RM | 1–6 reps |

### How to Estimate Your 1RM Without Testing It?
The Brzycki formula estimates your 1RM from a submaximal set:

**1RM ≈ Load × (36 / (37 − reps completed))**

Example: you lift 60 kg for 10 reps
→ 1RM ≈ 60 × (36 / 27) ≈ **80 kg**

> In Year 10 and Year 11, you don't need to test your actual 1RM. Estimating from a set of 10–12 reps is sufficient and safer.

## RIR — Reps In Reserve

> **The number of repetitions you could still perform before reaching muscle failure.**

### Definition
RIR (Reps In Reserve) is a **subjective estimate** of how close you are to muscle failure. It's an internal measure, based on how you feel at the end of a set.

- **RIR 0** = you've reached failure — impossible to do one more rep
- **RIR 1** = you could have done 1 more rep
- **RIR 3** = you could have done 3 more reps (set still comfortable)
- **RIR 5+** = set too easy, load insufficient

### What Is It For?
RIR lets you **regulate your load in real time**, without calculations. At the end of each set, evaluate your RIR and adjust:
- RIR > 4 → increase the load
- RIR 2–3 → optimal training zone
- RIR 0–1 → limit — use occasionally, not every set

> In Year 10, start by learning to recognize the burning sensation at the end of a set. It's the first indicator of RIR.

---

## RPE — Rating of Perceived Exertion

> **The subjective 1-to-10 scale that rates the overall difficulty felt during a set.**

### Definition
RPE (Rating of Perceived Exertion) is a **perceived exertion scale** from 1 to 10. Unlike RIR which counts remaining reps, RPE evaluates the **overall difficulty** of the set (muscular effort + cardio + mental).

| RPE | Feeling | Equivalent |
|-----|---------|------------|
| 1–3 | Very easy, warm-up | RIR 8+ |
| 4–5 | Moderate effort | RIR 5–7 |
| 6–7 | Notable effort, elevated breathing | RIR 3–4 |
| 8 | Hard, end of set noticeable | RIR 2–3 |
| 9 | Very hard, near-maximum | RIR 1 |
| 10 | Absolute maximum effort | RIR 0 |

### What Is It For?
RPE helps **self-regulate the overall intensity** of a session, taking into account daily fatigue, sleep, and stress. Two identical days can yield different RPE values for the same load.

## Comparison Table

| | RM | RIR | RPE |
|--|----|----|-----|
| **Nature** | Objective | Estimate | Subjective |
| **Focus** | Maximum load | Remaining reps | Overall perception |
| **When to use** | Load calculation | Regulate load per set | Evaluate the overall session |
| **Recommended level** | Year 11 / Year 12 | Year 10 (introduction) | Year 11 / Year 12 |

---

## Progression by Level

**Year 10:** learn to evaluate how you feel at the end of a set (intuitive RIR). Can you still do more reps? How many?

**Year 11:** use RM and RIR precisely to regulate your load from one set to the next.

**Year 12:** cross-reference all three indicators (objective RM + RIR + RPE) to analyze and adapt your training in real time, including accounting for how you feel that day.
